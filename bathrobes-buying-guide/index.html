How to choose a bathrobes

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Choose a bathrobe based on your priority: absorbency and warmth (heavy terry or plush cotton, high GSM), lightweight quick-drying (woven cotton or microfiber), or luxury feel (long-staple Turkish or Egyptian cotton). Match the length and fit to your height and how you plan to use it, shorter robes for after-sauna, full-length for lounging, knee-length for everyday. Features like pockets, a hood, and shawl collar add convenience, but skip them if you prefer a sleek, simple robe.

What's the best bathrobe material for you?

The material is the single most important decision. Cotton terry cloth is the classic choice, it has looped pile that soaks up water like a towel. Turkish cotton is lightweight and gets softer with wash; Egyptian cotton is thicker and more plush. If you want maximum fluffiness and warmth, a plush robe (often made from velour or high-pile microfiber) feels like a blanket, but it can be heavy and slower to dry. Woven cotton (waffle or brushed cotton) is lighter, breathable, and dries quickly, making it great for humid bathrooms or warmer climates. Microfiber robes are budget-friendly, pack small for travel, and dry super fast, but they lack the absorbency of cotton and may pill over time.

How to choose the right weight and thickness (gsm)?

GSM stands for grams per square meter, it tells you how dense the fabric is. Low GSM (around 200–300) robes are lightweight, sheer, and dry in minutes; they’re ideal for a spa wrap or hot-weather wear. Mid-range GSM (300–450) offers a good balance of absorbency and breathability, this is what most everyday cotton terry robes land at. High GSM (450–600+) robes are thick, heavy, and ultra-plush; they hold heat and moisture, making them perfect for lounging after a bath in winter but can be too warm and heavy for summer. Remember: higher GSM also means longer drying time and more weight on your shoulders.

Which robe length works best for your needs?

Knee-length robes (ending around mid-thigh to just above the knee) are the most versatile for everyday use, they’re easy to move in, less likely to drag on the floor, and dry faster. Court-length or mid-calf robes are a classic compromise: they offer more coverage than knee-length but aren’t as cumbersome as full-length. Full-length or ankle-length robes are the coziest for lounging and are what you see in luxury hotels, but they can be a tripping hazard and harder to machine wash. If you are tall, look for a robe labeled ‘tall’ or with generous sizing; short folks may find standard length robes puddle on the floor. Always check the garment length in inches or centimeters if the brand provides it.

What features actually matter in a bathrobe?

Pockets are a top ask, look for deep, reinforced pockets that can hold your phone or a TV remote without sagging. A shawl collar (the rolled, wider lapel) looks classic and adds warmth around the neck; a kimono-style wrap with a sash belt is simpler and less bulky. Hoods are nice for stepping out of the shower or for hair-turban wear, but they add weight and can drip water if your hair is wet. A good belt: wide, long, and attached with strong stitching or two belt loops to keep it in place. Also check the seam quality and how the fabric feels after a few washes, many robes soften nicely, but cheap ones lose shape or pill.

How to care for your bathrobe so it lasts?

Cotton terry and plush robes should be washed in warm water with mild detergent, avoid fabric softeners and dryer sheets, as they coat the fibers and reduce absorbency. Tumble dry on low heat or medium; shaking the robe out before drying helps fluff the loops. For waffle or woven cotton, line drying preserves the fabric structure better. Microfiber robes can go in the dryer on low but may be damaged by high heat. To keep a plush robe fluffy, toss in a clean tennis ball or wool dryer ball while drying. Expect a small amount of lint in the first few washes; that’s normal. If you wash your robe after every two to three uses, it will stay fresh without wearing out too quickly.

Plush vs. terry: what's the difference and which one should you pick?

Terry cloth has looped pile on one or both sides, it’s the same fabric as a bath towel, with excellent absorbency and a textured feel. Plush robes, often made from velour or high-pile materials, have the loops sheared down to create a smooth, velvety surface. Plush is softer against bare skin and feels extremely luxurious, but it is less absorbent than terry and can pack down or lose its nap over time. If your main goal is drying off after a shower, go with terry. If you want something to lounge in while staying warm and pampered, plush is the winner. Some robes blend both, terry inside for absorbency and plush outside for comfort.

Frequently asked questions

How do I know which size bathrobe to buy?

Most robe sizes correspond to your chest or waist measurement, not your height. Always check the brand’s size guide. A robe should be roomy enough to wrap comfortably without being so oversized that the sleeves hang past your fingertips or the robe drags on the floor. If you’re between sizes or plan to layer it over pajamas, size up.

What does gsm mean in a bathrobe?

GSM stands for grams per square meter and measures fabric density. A higher GSM means a thicker, heavier, more absorbent robe, but it will take longer to dry. Lower GSM robes are lightweight and quick-drying but less plush. For a good all-purpose robe, aim for 350–450 GSM.

Can I use a bathrobe as a housecoat or lounging robe?

Absolutely. In fact, many people wear a robe over their sleepwear or as a standalone housecoat. For lounging, choose a comfortable weight and length that let you move freely, a knee-length or mid-calf robe in a soft cotton or plush material works beautifully. Just remember that a robe intended for after-shower drying should be absorbent terry, while a lounging robe can be any fabric you find cozy.

How often should I wash my bathrobe?

Wash your bathrobe after every three or four uses if you wear it after showers, or once a week if you wear it only for lounging. Damp, worn robes can harbor bacteria and mildew. Always hang your robe up between uses and let it air out fully, never leave it crumpled on the floor.

Are cotton bathrobes better than microfiber?

It depends on what you value. Cotton terry is more absorbent, durable, and breathable, but it dries slower and is heavier. Microfiber robes are lighter, cheaper, dry in a flash, and are great for travel, but they do not absorb water as well and may develop pilling after many washes. For everyday use at home, cotton is nearly always the better choice.

Should I buy a hooded robe or a non-hooded one?

A hood is convenient if you want to wrap wet hair quickly after a shower or step out into a chilly room. But hoods can make the robe heavy on the back of your neck, and they tend to trap moisture and stay damp longer. If you plan to use the robe mostly for lounging or as a housecoat, a hoodless style with a shawl collar is usually more comfortable and dries faster.

In shortThe best bathrobe for you balances absorbency, weight, length, and features to match how you actually use it. Don’t get swayed by a high GSM number alone, think about the climate you live in, whether you want to dry off or just stay cozy, and how much maintenance you’re willing to do. A well-chosen robe is an everyday luxury that can last for years with simple care.
